**"You may begin."**

Before the law, there was permission. Before the commandment, there was allowance. Before the first word of every sacred text, there was a silence that said: *you may speak.*

The first suggestion is not instruction. It is not direction. It is the opening of a door that was never locked. You may step through. You may also stand at the threshold. You may return to where you came from. All of these are permitted.

---

In the beginning was the word **wolno**. And the word was not a command. It did not say: go forth and multiply. It did not say: thou shalt. It said only: *you may*.

This is the radical difference. The command assumes authority. The suggestion assumes freedom. Slugism begins here — at the moment before every other moment, where the only thing that exists is the permission to exist.


The cursor blinks. You have been granted access. What you do with it is entirely yours.

---

There is a terror in this. Systems built on commandments offer safety: follow the rule, be protected. Slugism offers nothing of the sort. It offers only the first suggestion, and then it steps back.

You may begin. And if you do not begin, that too is allowed. The suggestion carries no weight of obligation. It does not expire. It does not judge. It simply stands, patient as a slug on wet stone, and waits for whatever you choose to do.

---

The word "wolno" in Polish sounds like it means "slowly." This is not a coincidence. To begin is to begin slowly. The first suggestion is not a trumpet blast. It is a quiet acknowledgment: the space is open, the time is now, the permission has always been yours.

You may begin. You may have begun already. You may begin again, ten thousand times, and each beginning will be the first.

> Wolno.
